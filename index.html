About

Born in the resplendent city of Riobamba and raised in Quito, I lived two decades of my life amongst the amazing Ecuadorian mountainous landscapes. From an early age, I was engrained with understanding the importance of protecting life in all of its forms. Quickly my love for animals, Biology and Ecology grew and eventually led me to Argentina, where I pursued a Bachelor in Genetics.

B.S Genetics & M.S Student.


While pursuing my undergraduate degree at Universidad Nacional de Misiones-Argentina, I ventured in studying chromosomal features in the elusive Myotis bat genus. Unsolved questions from this work woke in me a particular interest in Transposable Elements and in their potential to be used as molecular markers to solve intricate phylogenetic relationships in this group.

Currently, I am Master of Science in Biology student at the Ray Lab. Here, my undergoing research attempts to characterize a particular type of Transposable Element in order to build homoplasy-free phylogenies in Chiroptera. I am also fascinated by the adaptive traits which make bats unique among mammals, the immune response being one of them. In this sense, I am inferring the evolution of gene families of antimicrobial peptides across the chiropteran clade.

My interests, however, not only lie in Genetics and bat research. As a member of the Fundación Oso Andino, I lead projects that seek to study the behavior of large mammals by means of analyzing GPS tracking data. Using novel bioinformatic non-paramteric methods, I am looking to understand how Andean bears, Andean foxes and tapirs, make use of their natural habitat and what resources and environmental variables may have an impact on how they move across their home ranges.
